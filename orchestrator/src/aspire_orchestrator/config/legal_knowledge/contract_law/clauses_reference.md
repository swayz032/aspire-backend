# Standard Contract Clauses Reference

## Indemnification

### Definition
An indemnification clause allocates risk between parties by requiring one party (indemnitor) to compensate the other (indemnitee) for specified losses, damages, or liabilities.

### Standard Language Pattern
"Party A shall indemnify, defend, and hold harmless Party B from and against any and all claims, damages, losses, costs, and expenses (including reasonable attorneys' fees) arising out of or relating to [specified scope]."

### Key Variations
- **Mutual indemnification:** Both parties indemnify each other for their own negligence/breach
- **One-way indemnification:** Only one party indemnifies (common in vendor agreements — vendor indemnifies client)
- **Broad form:** Indemnitor covers losses even for indemnitee's own negligence (prohibited in many states for construction contracts)
- **Intermediate form:** Indemnitor covers losses except for indemnitee's sole negligence
- **Limited form:** Indemnitor covers only losses caused by indemnitor's own negligence

### Red Flags
- One-sided indemnification in a supposedly mutual agreement
- Indemnification for the indemnitee's own negligence (especially in construction)
- No cap on indemnification liability
- Indemnification for consequential or punitive damages
- Failure to include "reasonable attorneys' fees" (shifts legal costs entirely to one party)

### Negotiation Tips
- Push for mutual indemnification whenever possible
- Cap indemnification at the contract value or a multiple thereof
- Exclude indemnification for the other party's negligence
- Ensure indemnitor has the right to control defense of claims
- Require prompt notice of claims triggering indemnification

## Limitation of Liability

### Definition
Limits the total amount one party can recover from the other, regardless of the cause of action.

### Standard Language Pattern
"In no event shall either party's total aggregate liability exceed the amounts paid or payable under this Agreement during the twelve (12) month period immediately preceding the claim."

### Common Cap Structures
- **Fixed dollar amount:** "Liability shall not exceed $50,000"
- **Contract value cap:** "Liability shall not exceed total fees paid under this Agreement"
- **Trailing period cap:** "Liability shall not exceed fees paid in the prior 12 months"
- **Multiple of fees:** "Liability shall not exceed 2x the fees paid"

### Carve-Outs (Exceptions to the Cap)
Certain liabilities are typically excluded from the cap:
- Indemnification obligations for third-party IP claims
- Breach of confidentiality
- Willful misconduct or gross negligence
- Fraud
- Death or bodily injury
- Breach of payment obligations

### Consequential Damages Waiver
Often paired with liability caps: "Neither party shall be liable for any indirect, incidental, special, consequential, or punitive damages, including lost profits."

### Red Flags
- No liability cap at all (unlimited exposure)
- Cap set unreasonably low relative to contract value
- Cap applies to one party but not the other
- No carve-outs for fraud or willful misconduct
- Waiver of consequential damages that effectively eliminates meaningful remedies

## Force Majeure

### Definition
Excuses performance when extraordinary events beyond a party's control prevent fulfillment of obligations.

### Standard Language Pattern
"Neither party shall be liable for any failure or delay in performance due to causes beyond its reasonable control, including but not limited to acts of God, fire, flood, earthquake, pandemic, epidemic, war, terrorism, government action, labor disputes, or supply chain disruption."

### Key Elements
- **Triggering events:** Enumerated list (courts interpret narrowly — only listed events qualify)
- **Catch-all:** "and other events beyond reasonable control" (varies by jurisdiction in enforceability)
- **Notice requirement:** Party must notify the other within X days of the force majeure event
- **Mitigation duty:** Affected party must use reasonable efforts to mitigate
- **Duration limit:** If force majeure continues beyond X months, either party may terminate
- **Partial performance:** Party must continue performing obligations not affected by the event

### Post-COVID Considerations
After COVID-19, force majeure clauses have become more scrutinized:
- "Pandemic" and "epidemic" are now commonly enumerated
- "Government orders," "quarantine," and "public health emergency" added to many templates
- Courts distinguish between events that make performance impossible vs. merely more expensive
- Supply chain disruption increasingly accepted as force majeure trigger
- Remote work capability may undermine claims of impossibility for service contracts

### Red Flags
- No force majeure clause at all (leaves parties exposed)
- Force majeure only benefits one party
- No notice requirement (allows post-hoc claims)
- No termination right after extended force majeure
- Overly broad catch-all that could be invoked for economic downturns (not true force majeure)

## Termination

### Definition
Specifies the conditions, process, and consequences of ending the contract before its natural expiration.

### Common Termination Types
1. **Termination for convenience:** Either party can terminate with X days' written notice, for any reason
2. **Termination for cause:** Termination upon material breach, with cure period
3. **Termination for insolvency:** Immediate termination if a party files for bankruptcy or becomes insolvent
4. **Termination upon regulatory change:** If law changes make performance illegal or impractical

### Standard Cure Period Pattern
"If either party materially breaches this Agreement, the non-breaching party shall provide written notice specifying the breach. The breaching party shall have thirty (30) days to cure. If the breach is not cured within the cure period, the non-breaching party may terminate immediately."

### Post-Termination Obligations
- Payment of outstanding invoices
- Return or destruction of confidential information
- Survival of specified clauses (indemnification, confidentiality, limitation of liability)
- Transition assistance period
- Accrued rights and obligations preserved

### Red Flags
- Only one party has termination rights
- No cure period for breach (immediate termination)
- Termination penalties disproportionate to damages
- No survival clause (leaves post-termination obligations ambiguous)
- Auto-renewal with difficult cancellation process

## Confidentiality / Non-Disclosure

### Definition
Obligates parties to protect confidential information shared during the contract.

### Standard Elements
- **Definition of confidential information:** What is covered (trade secrets, business plans, financials, customer data, technical information)
- **Exclusions:** Publicly known information, independently developed information, information received from third parties without restriction, information required by law/court order
- **Permitted use:** Information may only be used for purposes of the agreement
- **Permitted disclosure:** To employees, contractors, advisors with need-to-know and bound by similar obligations
- **Return/destruction:** Upon termination, return or destroy all confidential materials
- **Survival period:** Confidentiality obligations survive for X years after termination (typically 2-5 years; trade secrets: indefinite)

### Red Flags
- Overly broad definition of confidential information (everything is confidential)
- No exclusions for publicly available information
- No carve-out for legally compelled disclosure
- Survival period too short (< 2 years) or perpetual (potentially unenforceable in some contexts)
- No requirement to return materials upon termination

## Non-Compete

### Definition
Restricts a party from competing with the other party during and after the contract term.

### Enforceability Factors (Varies Significantly by State)
Courts evaluate reasonableness based on:
1. **Scope of activity:** Must be narrowly tailored to specific competitive activities
2. **Geographic scope:** Must be limited to areas where the business actually operates
3. **Duration:** Typically 1-2 years maximum; longer periods face increased scrutiny
4. **Consideration:** Must be supported by adequate consideration (employment alone may suffice for new hires; existing employees often need additional consideration)

### State-Specific Rules
- **California:** Non-competes are generally void and unenforceable (Business and Professions Code 16600). Exception: sale of business goodwill
- **FTC proposed rule (2024):** Proposed nationwide ban on most non-competes — currently in litigation; not yet final
- **Many states trending toward restriction:** Colorado, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, Oregon, Washington limit non-competes for low-wage workers

### Red Flags
- Geographic scope broader than business operations
- Duration exceeding 2 years without strong justification
- Scope covering industries unrelated to the contract
- Applied to independent contractors (disfavored in many jurisdictions)
- No consideration beyond continued employment for existing employees

## Intellectual Property Ownership

### Work-for-Hire Doctrine
Under copyright law, work created by an employee within the scope of employment belongs to the employer. For independent contractors, the work belongs to the contractor UNLESS there is a written agreement assigning IP rights.

### Assignment Clause Pattern
"Contractor hereby assigns to Company all right, title, and interest in and to any and all work product, deliverables, inventions, and intellectual property created in connection with this Agreement."

### License-Back Provision
If contractor retains IP ownership, company receives a license: "Contractor grants Company a perpetual, irrevocable, worldwide, royalty-free, non-exclusive license to use, modify, and distribute the deliverables."

### Red Flags
- No IP ownership clause (ambiguous ownership)
- Assignment of pre-existing IP without limitation
- No license-back if contractor retains ownership
- Assignment of IP created outside the scope of the contract
- No representation that work is original and non-infringing

## Dispute Resolution

### Litigation
Traditional court-based dispute resolution. Parties specify:
- **Governing law:** Which state's law applies
- **Venue/jurisdiction:** Which court(s) have jurisdiction
- **Exclusive vs. non-exclusive:** Whether specified courts are the only option

### Arbitration
Private dispute resolution by neutral arbitrator(s). Common in commercial contracts.
- **Administering body:** AAA (American Arbitration Association), JAMS, or ad hoc
- **Rules:** Commercial Arbitration Rules, Employment Rules, Construction Rules
- **Number of arbitrators:** One (smaller disputes) or three (larger disputes)
- **Binding vs. non-binding:** Most commercial arbitration is binding
- **Location:** Specified city/state
- **Discovery limitations:** Typically more limited than litigation

### Mediation
Non-binding facilitated negotiation. Often required as a first step before arbitration or litigation.

### Escalation Ladder Pattern
"Disputes shall first be submitted to good-faith negotiation between executives. If unresolved within 30 days, the dispute shall be submitted to mediation under AAA rules. If mediation fails within 60 days, the dispute shall be resolved by binding arbitration."

### Red Flags
- No dispute resolution clause (defaults to litigation in any competent court)
- Mandatory arbitration with one-sided terms
- Arbitration in a distant or inconvenient location
- Party paying all arbitration costs regardless of outcome
- Waiver of right to jury trial without clear disclosure

## Governing Law

### Purpose
Specifies which state's (or country's) laws will be used to interpret the contract.

### Standard Pattern
"This Agreement shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of [State], without regard to its conflict of laws principles."

### Why "Without Regard to Conflict of Laws"
This phrase prevents courts from applying another state's law under conflict of laws analysis. Without it, a court might determine that another state's law should apply based on the parties' locations or where performance occurs.

## Severability

### Purpose
If a court finds one clause invalid or unenforceable, the rest of the contract remains in effect.

### Standard Pattern
"If any provision of this Agreement is held invalid or unenforceable, the remaining provisions shall continue in full force and effect."

### Enhanced Severability
"If any provision is found invalid, the parties shall negotiate in good faith to replace it with a valid provision that achieves the original intent as closely as possible."

## Entire Agreement (Integration Clause)

### Purpose
States that the written contract is the complete agreement, superseding all prior negotiations, representations, and agreements.

### Standard Pattern
"This Agreement constitutes the entire agreement between the parties and supersedes all prior agreements, understandings, and representations, whether written or oral."

### Effect
Prevents parties from introducing evidence of prior oral agreements or side deals (parol evidence rule).

## Amendment

### Purpose
Specifies how the contract can be modified.

### Standard Pattern
"This Agreement may not be amended or modified except by a written instrument signed by both parties."

### Importance
Without an amendment clause, oral modifications might be enforceable in some jurisdictions. The written amendment requirement prevents disputes about alleged verbal changes.

## Assignment

### Purpose
Specifies whether and how parties can transfer their rights and obligations to third parties.

### Standard Pattern
"Neither party may assign this Agreement without the prior written consent of the other party, which consent shall not be unreasonably withheld."

### Anti-Assignment Exception
"Notwithstanding the foregoing, either party may assign this Agreement to an affiliate or in connection with a merger, acquisition, or sale of all or substantially all of its assets."

## Notices

### Purpose
Specifies how formal communications (termination, breach, etc.) must be delivered.

### Standard Pattern
"All notices shall be in writing and deemed given when: (a) delivered personally; (b) sent by certified mail, return receipt requested; (c) sent by recognized overnight courier; or (d) sent by email with confirmation of receipt."

### Key Details
- Must specify addresses for each party
- Should include a method to update notice addresses
- Email notice increasingly accepted but should be supplemented with another method for critical notices

## Waiver

### Purpose
Clarifies that failure to enforce a right does not waive it for the future.

### Standard Pattern
"No waiver of any provision shall be deemed a waiver of any other provision or a continuing waiver. Any waiver must be in writing and signed by the waiving party."
