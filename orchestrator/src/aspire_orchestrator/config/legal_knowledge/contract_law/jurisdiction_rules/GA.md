# Georgia Contract Law — Key Variations

## E-Signature Rules
Georgia adopted UETA (OCGA 10-12-1 through 10-12-20, effective 2009). Electronic signatures have full legal equivalence.

**Specific rules:**
- Georgia also enacted the Georgia Electronic Records and Signatures Act (OCGA 10-12-1) prior to UETA adoption
- Remote online notarization (RON) permitted under SB 188 (effective July 2022)
- Georgia courts have upheld e-signatures in real estate transactions and commercial contexts

## Non-Compete Agreements
Georgia enacted the Restrictive Covenant Act (OCGA 13-8-50 through 13-8-59, effective May 2011), which significantly changed non-compete enforcement.

**Key features (post-2011):**
- Non-competes are enforceable if reasonable in time, geographic area, and scope of prohibited activities
- Courts may modify (blue-pencil) overly broad covenants — this was NOT permitted under prior Georgia law
- Maximum duration: 2 years for employment agreements; 5 years for sale of business
- Geographic scope: Must specify territories, not just "wherever company does business"
- Consideration: Employment alone is sufficient for new hires; for existing employees, continued employment for a "substantial period" or other consideration

**Pre-2011 contracts:** Governed by the old Georgia common law, which was extremely hostile to non-competes (courts would not blue-pencil and required very precise drafting).

## Statute of Limitations for Contract Disputes
- **Written contracts:** 6 years (OCGA 9-3-24)
- **Oral contracts:** 4 years (OCGA 9-3-25)
- **Breach of warranty (goods):** 4 years (UCC)
- **Fraud:** 4 years from discovery (OCGA 9-3-96)
- **Professional malpractice:** 2 years from discovery, max 5 years from act (OCGA 9-3-97.1 for medical; 4 years for legal)

## Notice Requirements
- **Residential lease termination (month-to-month):** 60 days by landlord; 30 days by tenant (OCGA 44-7-7)
- **Eviction (non-payment):** Demand for payment, then dispossessory affidavit filed with court
- **Commercial lease:** Per lease terms

## Georgia-Specific Contract Rules

### Security Deposit (Residential)
- **Maximum:** No statutory limit
- **Return deadline:** 30 days after tenant vacates (OCGA 44-7-34)
- **Itemized deductions required** with written statement
- **Move-in inspection required:** Landlord must conduct and document (OCGA 44-7-33)
- **Escrow:** Not required for landlords with fewer than 10 units; required for 10+ units (must place in escrow or post surety bond)
- **Penalty:** Bad faith retention may result in liability for 3x the amount wrongfully withheld

### Contractor Licensing
Georgia has statewide contractor licensing through the Georgia Secretary of State:
- **General Contractor license required** for projects $2,500+
- **Residential Basic Contractor:** Projects under $100,000 (single trade) or $300,000 (general)
- **Residential Light Commercial:** Up to $600,000
- **General Contractor (Unlimited):** No project cap
- **Separate licenses:** Electrical, plumbing, conditioned air (HVAC), low voltage
- Working without a license: Criminal misdemeanor; contracts may be unenforceable

### Mechanic's Lien (OCGA 44-14-360 et seq.)
- **Preliminary notice:** Not required
- **Lien filing deadline:** 90 days from completion of work (OCGA 44-14-361.1)
- **Notice of filing:** Must serve written notice on owner within 2 business days of filing
- **Foreclosure deadline:** 12 months from filing
- **Statutory lien bond:** Owner can bond off lien

### Georgia Fair Business Practices Act (OCGA 10-1-390)
- Prohibits unfair or deceptive practices in consumer transactions
- Private right of action for actual damages; treble damages for intentional violations
- Attorney fees recoverable
- Written demand required 30 days before filing suit

### Restrictive Covenant Act — Additional Details
- Applies to employment agreements, franchise agreements, and sale of business
- Non-solicitation (customer and employee) agreements treated separately from non-competes
- Confidentiality covenants are explicitly authorized and not subject to time/geography limits
- In terrorem clause (forfeiture provisions for violating covenant) permitted
