# North Carolina Contract Law — Key Variations

## E-Signature Rules
North Carolina adopted UETA (NCGS Chapter 66, Article 40, effective 2000). Electronic signatures are legally equivalent to wet-ink signatures.

**Specific rules:**
- North Carolina also enacted the Uniform Electronic Commerce Act (NCGS 66-311 through 66-330)
- Remote online notarization (RON) permitted under NCGS 10B-134.1 et seq. (effective July 2023 for permanent program; emergency RON was available during COVID)
- E-notarization available for both NC residents and out-of-state signers

## Non-Compete Agreements
North Carolina enforces non-competes under common law reasonableness analysis. No specific statute.

**Enforceability factors:**
- Must be in writing and part of a contract
- Must be supported by valuable consideration (employment is sufficient for new hires; continued employment is sufficient for existing employees)
- Must be reasonable in time (typically 1-2 years for employees; 3-5 years for sale of business)
- Must be reasonable in geographic scope
- Must not be against public policy

**North Carolina-specific:**
- Courts strictly construe non-competes and will NOT blue-pencil — if any part is unreasonable, the entire covenant is void
- This strict approach means drafting must be precise — an overly broad clause invalidates the whole provision
- Non-solicitation agreements (customer and employee) are evaluated separately but under similar reasonableness standard

## Statute of Limitations for Contract Disputes
- **Written contracts:** 3 years (NCGS 1-52(1)) — shorter than many states
- **Oral contracts:** 3 years (NCGS 1-52(1))
- **Breach of warranty (goods):** 4 years (UCC)
- **Fraud:** 3 years from discovery (NCGS 1-52(9))
- **Professional malpractice:** 3 years from act, or 1 year from discovery, max 4 years from act (NCGS 1-15(c))

## Notice Requirements
- **Residential lease termination (month-to-month):** 7 days (NCGS 42-14) — notably short
- **Week-to-week tenancy:** 2 days (NCGS 42-14)
- **Eviction (non-payment):** 10-day notice (NCGS 42-3)
- **Commercial lease:** Per lease terms

## North Carolina-Specific Contract Rules

### Security Deposit (Residential)
- **Maximum:** 1.5 months' rent for month-to-month; 2 months' rent for longer leases (NCGS 42-51)
- **Pet deposit:** Additional reasonable pet deposit allowed
- **Return deadline:** 30 days after tenant vacates (NCGS 42-52)
- **Itemized deductions required**
- **Trust account required:** Landlord must hold deposit in trust account in NC banking institution OR furnish bond
- **Interest:** Not required unless landlord elects to pay interest on deposits

### Contractor Licensing
North Carolina requires contractor licensing through the NC Licensing Board for General Contractors:
- License required for projects $30,000+ (including labor and materials)
- Categories: Building, Residential, Unclassified, Public Utilities
- Separate licenses: Electrical (NC Board of Examiners of Electrical Contractors), Plumbing/HVAC (NC Board of Examiners of Plumbing, Heating and Fire Sprinkler Contractors)
- Unlicensed contracting: Misdemeanor; contract may be unenforceable

### Mechanic's Lien (NCGS Chapter 44A)
- **Notice to lien agent:** Required for projects $30,000+ — must designate a lien agent; subs/suppliers must identify themselves to lien agent within 15 days of first furnishing
- **Subcontractor notice of claim:** Must be filed with property owner within 120 days of last furnishing
- **Lien filing deadline:** 120 days from last furnishing
- **Foreclosure deadline:** 180 days from last furnishing
- **Unique:** North Carolina's lien agent system is distinctive — most other states do not require it

### Unfair and Deceptive Trade Practices Act (NCGS 75-1.1)
- Broad prohibition on unfair or deceptive acts in commerce
- Private right of action with treble damages for violations
- Attorney fees recoverable
- Widely used in commercial disputes (not just consumer claims)
- Business-to-business claims are actionable under this statute

### Employment Classification
North Carolina follows the IRS common law test for worker classification. The NC Department of Revenue actively investigates misclassification, particularly in the construction industry.
