# New York Contract Law — Key Variations

## E-Signature Rules
New York did NOT adopt UETA. Instead, New York enacted the Electronic Signatures and Records Act (ESRA, State Technology Law Article 3, effective 2000).

**ESRA vs. UETA key differences:**
- ESRA is technology-neutral — does not prescribe specific e-signature technologies
- ESRA applies to government agencies as well as private parties
- Consumer consent requirements are similar to ESIGN Act
- New York courts have upheld e-signatures under ESRA in commercial contexts

**Remote online notarization:** New York permits RON (effective February 2023, extended from COVID-era executive orders). Requires notary to use approved technology platform.

## Non-Compete Agreements
New York currently enforces non-competes under a reasonableness standard but has been actively legislating restrictions.

**Current enforceability factors:**
- Must protect a legitimate business interest (trade secrets, client relationships, unique services)
- Must be reasonable in time (typically 1-2 years)
- Must be reasonable in geographic scope
- Must not impose undue hardship on the employee
- Must not harm the public interest
- Consideration required (new employment suffices for new hires; additional consideration needed for existing employees)

**Blue-pencil doctrine:** New York courts may modify (blue-pencil) overly broad non-competes to make them enforceable rather than voiding them entirely.

**Recent legislative activity:** NY legislature has passed bills to ban non-competes (e.g., A1278B/S3100A in 2023), but they were vetoed by the Governor. Watch for future legislation.

## Statute of Limitations for Contract Disputes
- **Written contracts:** 6 years (CPLR 213(2))
- **Oral contracts:** 6 years (CPLR 213(2))
- **Breach of warranty (goods):** 4 years (UCC 2-725)
- **Fraud:** 6 years from commission or 2 years from discovery, whichever is longer (CPLR 213(8), 203(g))
- **Professional malpractice (non-medical):** 3 years (CPLR 214(6))

## Notice Requirements
- **Residential lease termination (month-to-month):** 30 days (outside NYC); inside NYC, varies by tenancy length: 30 days (under 1 year), 60 days (1-2 years), 90 days (over 2 years) under HSTPA
- **Rent increase notice:** Same tiered structure as termination under HSTPA
- **Commercial lease:** Per lease terms; no statutory minimum

## New York-Specific Contract Rules

### Security Deposit (Residential)
- **Maximum:** 1 month's rent (HSTPA 2019 eliminated higher deposits)
- **Return deadline:** 14 days after tenant vacates
- **Itemized deductions required** with receipts
- **Must be held in interest-bearing account** in NY banking institution (for buildings with 6+ units)
- **Cannot use last month's rent as security deposit**

### Contractor Licensing
New York State does not have a statewide general contractor license requirement. However, NYC requires licensing through the NYC Department of Buildings for specific trades (plumbing, electrical, fire suppression). Other municipalities have their own requirements.

### General Obligations Law
- **GOL 5-701 (Statute of Frauds):** Contracts that cannot be performed within one year, guaranty agreements, and contracts for sale of goods >$500 must be in writing
- **GOL 5-1401:** Parties may choose New York law if transaction involves $250,000+ (no reasonable relationship to NY required)
- **GOL 5-1402:** Parties may consent to NY jurisdiction if transaction involves $1,000,000+
- **GOL 15-108:** Anti-indemnification statute for construction — prohibits broad-form indemnification (cannot indemnify for your own negligence)

### Mechanic's Lien
- **Preliminary notice:** Not required in most cases (exception: sub-subcontractors on private projects)
- **Lien filing deadline:** 8 months from last furnishing (private projects); 30 days after filing for public improvement
- **Foreclosure deadline:** 1 year from filing
- **Lien bond:** Owner can discharge lien by posting surety bond equal to 110% of lien amount

### Rent Stabilization (NYC)
If the property is rent-stabilized, lease terms are subject to Rent Guidelines Board orders. Clara should flag if a residential lease in NYC may be subject to rent stabilization.
