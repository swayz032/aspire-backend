# Contract Types Reference

## Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)

### Mutual NDA
Both parties agree to protect each other's confidential information. Used when both sides are sharing sensitive business information.

**Common scenarios for small business:**
- Exploring a partnership or joint venture
- Discussing merger or acquisition
- Vendor evaluating proprietary processes
- Two businesses exploring collaboration

**Key terms:**
- Definition of confidential information (broad but with standard exclusions)
- Permitted use: only for evaluating the potential business relationship
- Term: typically 1-3 years for the NDA itself; confidentiality obligations survive 2-5 years after expiration
- Return/destruction of materials upon termination
- No license grant implied by disclosure

### Unilateral (One-Way) NDA
Only one party discloses confidential information. The receiving party is bound; the disclosing party is not.

**Common scenarios:**
- Hiring a contractor who will access business systems
- Pitching to an investor (investor receives confidential business plan)
- Sharing proprietary methods with a vendor for quoting purposes

**Key difference from mutual:** Only one party has obligations. Simpler, but less protection for the receiving party who may inadvertently share their own information.

### NDA Duration Guidelines
- **Short-term project:** 1 year NDA term, 2 year confidentiality survival
- **Ongoing relationship:** Perpetual during relationship, 3 year survival after termination
- **Trade secrets:** Indefinite confidentiality obligation (matches trade secret protection duration)

## Master Service Agreement (MSA)

### Purpose
Establishes the overarching terms governing an ongoing business relationship. Individual projects are executed under Statements of Work (SOWs) that reference the MSA.

### Structure
- **MSA:** General terms (payment, liability, IP, termination, dispute resolution)
- **SOW(s):** Specific project scope, deliverables, timeline, pricing
- **Hierarchy clause:** In case of conflict, MSA governs unless SOW explicitly states otherwise

### Key MSA Sections
1. Definitions
2. Scope of services (general; specific scope in SOWs)
3. Payment terms (general framework; specific pricing in SOWs)
4. Intellectual property ownership
5. Confidentiality
6. Representations and warranties
7. Indemnification
8. Limitation of liability
9. Term and termination
10. Insurance requirements
11. Independent contractor status
12. Dispute resolution
13. General provisions (governing law, severability, entire agreement, amendment)

### When to Use MSA + SOW vs. Standalone Contract
- **MSA + SOW:** Multiple projects over time with the same client/vendor. Negotiate terms once, execute quickly for each project.
- **Standalone contract:** One-off engagement, no expectation of ongoing relationship.

## Statement of Work (SOW)

### Purpose
Defines the specific deliverables, timeline, milestones, and pricing for a project under an MSA.

### Key SOW Elements
1. Project description and objectives
2. Scope of work (detailed)
3. Deliverables (specific, measurable)
4. Acceptance criteria for each deliverable
5. Timeline and milestones
6. Pricing and payment schedule (fixed-fee, T&M, milestone-based)
7. Assumptions and dependencies
8. Change order process
9. Project contacts and escalation

### SOW Best Practices
- Be specific about what is IN scope and what is OUT of scope
- Define acceptance criteria objectively (measurable outcomes, not subjective satisfaction)
- Include a change order process for scope changes
- Reference the MSA for general terms: "This SOW is governed by the MSA dated [date]"

## Independent Contractor Agreement

### Purpose
Establishes the relationship between a business and an independent contractor (1099 worker). Critical for proper worker classification.

### Key Provisions
1. **Independent contractor status:** Explicit statement that contractor is not an employee
2. **Control:** Company controls WHAT is done (deliverables) but NOT HOW (methods, tools, schedule)
3. **Tools and equipment:** Contractor provides their own
4. **Payment:** By project/invoice, not salary; no benefits, no withholding
5. **Insurance:** Contractor maintains their own liability insurance
6. **IP assignment:** Work product belongs to company (must be explicit for non-employees)
7. **Termination:** Either party can terminate with notice; no severance
8. **Non-solicitation:** Contractor cannot solicit company's clients/employees during and after term

### Worker Classification Risk Factors
IRS and state agencies examine the actual relationship, not just the contract label. Red flags for misclassification:
- Company controls when, where, and how work is done
- Contractor works exclusively for one company
- Company provides tools, equipment, workspace
- Contractor receives regular pay vs. project-based
- Relationship is indefinite rather than project-based
- Contractor performs core business functions

**Consequences of misclassification:**
- Back taxes (employer and employee portions) plus penalties
- Overtime and benefits claims
- Workers' compensation exposure
- State fines and penalties
- In California: additional penalties under AB 5

## Employment Agreement

### Key Differences from Contractor Agreement
- Employer controls methods and manner of work
- Employer withholds taxes (W-2)
- Employee receives benefits (if applicable)
- At-will employment presumed (except Montana) unless contract specifies otherwise
- Non-compete enforceability varies significantly by state

### Typical Employment Agreement Sections
1. Position and duties
2. Compensation (salary, bonus, equity)
3. Benefits
4. At-will statement (or term of employment)
5. Confidentiality and IP assignment
6. Non-compete and non-solicitation
7. Termination provisions
8. Severance (if applicable)

## Lease Agreement — Residential

### Key Provisions
1. **Parties:** Landlord, tenant(s)
2. **Premises:** Property address and description
3. **Term:** Start date, end date, renewal options
4. **Rent:** Monthly amount, due date, late fees, grace period, acceptable payment methods
5. **Security deposit:** Amount, conditions for return, itemization requirements, interest (state-specific)
6. **Utilities:** Which are included, which tenant pays
7. **Maintenance:** Landlord vs. tenant responsibilities
8. **Rules:** Pet policy, noise, smoking, parking, guest policy
9. **Entry:** Landlord's right to enter, notice requirements (typically 24-48 hours, state-specific)
10. **Termination:** Notice requirements for non-renewal, early termination penalties
11. **Habitability:** Landlord's obligation to maintain habitable conditions
12. **Disclosures:** Lead paint (pre-1978), mold, sex offender registry, flood zone (state-specific)

### Residential Lease — State Variations
Security deposit limits, return timelines, required disclosures, and eviction procedures vary dramatically by state. Clara MUST check jurisdiction-specific rules before generating residential leases.

## Lease Agreement — Commercial

### Key Differences from Residential
- Far fewer tenant protections (no habitability warranty in most states)
- More complex rent structures (base rent + CAM + percentage rent)
- Tenant improvements (TI) negotiation
- Assignment and subletting provisions more detailed
- Personal guarantee often required for small business tenants
- Triple-net (NNN) vs. gross vs. modified gross lease structures
- HVAC, roof, and structural maintenance allocation

## Engagement Letter (Professional Services)

### Purpose
Establishes the scope, fees, and terms for professional services (accounting, consulting, legal). Required by professional standards for CPAs (AICPA) and attorneys.

### Key Sections
1. **Scope of services:** Specific services to be performed (e.g., monthly bookkeeping, tax preparation, audit)
2. **Out-of-scope services:** Explicitly excluded services (critical to manage expectations)
3. **Client responsibilities:** Documents to provide, deadlines, access to systems
4. **Fees:** Hourly rate, fixed fee, retainer; billing frequency; payment terms
5. **Term:** Engagement period; renewal terms
6. **Confidentiality:** Ethical and contractual obligations
7. **Limitation of liability:** Often capped at fees paid
8. **Termination:** Notice period; disposition of records
9. **Professional standards:** Applicable standards (GAAP, GAAS, SSARS)

### CPA-Specific Requirements (SAS 136 / SSARS)
- Engagement letter required for all attest services
- Must specify management's responsibilities
- Must specify auditor's responsibilities
- Must be signed before work begins
- Must be updated when scope changes

## Subcontractor Agreement

### Purpose
Governs the relationship between a general contractor (GC) and a subcontractor performing specific trade work.

### Key Provisions
1. **Scope of work:** Specific trade or portion of the project
2. **Schedule:** Start date, milestones, completion deadline; liquidated damages for delay
3. **Pricing:** Fixed price, unit pricing, or T&M; retainage percentage (typically 5-10%)
4. **Payment:** Pay-when-paid or pay-if-paid clauses; progress payment schedule
5. **Insurance:** Required coverages and limits (GL, workers' comp, auto); additional insured endorsement
6. **Change orders:** Process for approving scope/price changes
7. **Warranty:** Workmanship warranty period (typically 1-2 years)
8. **Lien rights:** Mechanic's lien waivers (conditional vs. unconditional)
9. **Indemnification:** Sub indemnifies GC for sub's negligence
10. **Termination:** For cause (defective work, delay) and for convenience
11. **Compliance:** Building codes, permits, OSHA, local regulations
12. **Cleanup:** Daily cleanup and final cleanup obligations

### Pay-When-Paid vs. Pay-If-Paid
- **Pay-when-paid:** GC pays sub within reasonable time after GC receives payment from owner. Timing mechanism only — GC must eventually pay.
- **Pay-if-paid:** GC only pays sub IF GC receives payment from owner. True condition precedent — if owner doesn't pay, sub bears the risk. Some states void pay-if-paid clauses as against public policy.
