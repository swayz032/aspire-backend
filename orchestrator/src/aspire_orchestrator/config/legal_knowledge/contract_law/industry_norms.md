# Industry-Specific Contract Norms

## Trades & Construction

### Mechanic's Lien Rights
A mechanic's lien gives contractors, subcontractors, and material suppliers a security interest in the property they improved, allowing them to force sale of the property if unpaid.

**Lien filing requirements (general — state-specific):**
- Preliminary notice: Most states require a notice to property owner within 20-30 days of starting work or first material delivery
- Lien filing deadline: Typically 60-120 days after completion or last furnishing of labor/materials
- Foreclosure deadline: Typically 6-12 months after lien filing
- Must be filed in the county recorder's office where the property is located

**Lien waiver types:**
- **Conditional waiver on progress payment:** Waives lien rights for work performed through a specific date, conditioned on payment actually clearing
- **Unconditional waiver on progress payment:** Immediately waives lien rights for work through a date, regardless of whether payment clears (use with caution)
- **Conditional waiver on final payment:** Waives all remaining lien rights, conditioned on final payment clearing
- **Unconditional waiver on final payment:** Immediately waives all lien rights (only sign after final payment confirmed)

**Aspire implication:** Clara should include lien waiver language in construction contracts and flag when preliminary notice deadlines are approaching.

### Change Orders
Formal modifications to the original scope, price, or schedule of a construction contract.

**Required elements:**
- Description of changed work
- Reason for change (owner request, unforeseen condition, design error)
- Cost impact (additions, deductions, or no change)
- Schedule impact (days added, no change)
- Signatures of both parties BEFORE changed work begins
- Reference to original contract and prior change orders

**Common disputes:**
- Verbal change orders (unenforceable under many contracts)
- Disputed costs for changed work
- Schedule extensions — contractor claims delay, owner disputes
- Cumulative impact of multiple small changes

### Retainage
A percentage of each progress payment withheld by the owner or GC until project completion.

**Standard retainage:**
- Typical rate: 5-10% of each progress payment
- Released upon substantial completion and final punch list completion
- Some contracts reduce retainage to 5% after 50% completion
- Some states cap retainage percentages (e.g., Texas: 10% max)

**Retainage disputes:**
- Release delayed by punch list items
- Subcontractor retainage held even after sub's work is complete
- Interest on held retainage (required in some states)

### Construction Contract Types
- **Fixed price (lump sum):** Contractor bears cost risk; change orders for scope changes
- **Cost-plus:** Owner pays actual costs plus fee (fixed fee or percentage); cost overrun risk on owner
- **Cost-plus with GMP (Guaranteed Maximum Price):** Hybrid — owner pays costs up to a cap; contractor bears excess cost risk
- **Unit price:** Payment per unit of work (linear feet, cubic yards); quantity risk on owner
- **Time and materials:** Hourly rate plus materials at cost; highest owner risk for cost overrun

### Insurance Requirements for Trades
Standard insurance requirements in construction subcontracts:
- **Commercial General Liability (CGL):** $1M per occurrence, $2M aggregate minimum
- **Workers' Compensation:** Statutory limits (required by law in most states)
- **Automobile Liability:** $1M combined single limit
- **Umbrella/Excess:** $1M-$5M depending on project size
- **Professional Liability (E&O):** Required for design professionals
- **Builder's Risk:** Typically carried by owner or GC, covering property damage during construction
- **Additional Insured endorsement:** GC and owner named as additional insured on sub's CGL policy

## Accounting / Bookkeeping

### Engagement Letter Requirements
Professional standards (AICPA SSARS, SAS 136) require written engagement letters for:
- Compilation engagements
- Review engagements
- Audit engagements
- Tax preparation
- Agreed-upon procedures

**Minimum engagement letter content:**
- Objective and scope of services
- Responsibilities of the accountant
- Responsibilities of management/client
- Limitations of the engagement
- Identification of applicable financial reporting framework (GAAP, IFRS, tax basis)
- Expected form and content of reports
- Fee arrangement

### Professional Liability Considerations
- CPAs carry professional liability (E&O) insurance
- Engagement letters typically cap liability at fees paid
- Disclaimer of responsibility for fraud detection (unless audit engagement)
- Client responsible for accuracy of information provided
- Retention of working papers (typically 7 years; varies by state)

### Client Data Access Authorization
Bookkeeping/accounting engagements often require access to:
- Bank accounts (read-only via Plaid or similar)
- Accounting software (QuickBooks, Xero)
- Payroll systems
- Point-of-sale systems
- Document storage (receipts, invoices)

**Authorization should specify:**
- Systems accessed and access level (read-only, read-write)
- Duration of access (term of engagement only)
- Revocation process
- Data handling and confidentiality
- Liability for system errors

### SAS 136 Engagement Letter Changes (Effective Dec 2021)
SAS 136 (Forming an Opinion and Reporting on Financial Statements) introduced requirements:
- Engagement letter must describe auditor's responsibilities for detecting fraud
- Must describe group audit communications (for group audits)
- Must reference predecessor auditor communications
- Management representations letter tied to engagement letter scope

## Property Management

### Fair Housing Act Compliance
Federal Fair Housing Act (42 USC 3601-3619) prohibits discrimination in housing based on:
- Race
- Color
- National origin
- Religion
- Sex (including sexual orientation and gender identity per Bostock v. Clayton County application)
- Familial status (families with children under 18)
- Disability

**Lease provisions that violate Fair Housing:**
- Age restrictions (except qualified senior housing: 55+ or 62+ communities)
- Occupancy limits that discriminate against families (unreasonably low per-room limits)
- Pet policies that don't accommodate assistance animals
- Parking or amenity access that discriminates

**Reasonable accommodations:**
- Landlord must allow modifications for disabled tenants (at tenant's expense for private housing)
- Assistance animals are NOT pets — cannot charge pet deposit or pet rent
- Must accommodate emotional support animals with proper documentation

### Security Deposit Rules
Highly state-specific. Key variables:
- **Maximum deposit amount:** Some states cap at 1-2 months' rent (e.g., CA: 1 month unfurnished, 2 months furnished effective July 2024)
- **Interest requirement:** Some states require deposits be held in interest-bearing accounts (e.g., IL for buildings with 25+ units, NJ, CT)
- **Return timeline:** Ranges from 14 days (HI) to 60 days (AL) after move-out
- **Itemized statement:** Most states require itemized list of deductions with receipts
- **Walk-through rights:** Some states require pre-move-out walk-through with tenant
- **Penalties for non-compliance:** Many states impose 2x-3x deposit penalties for late return

### Eviction Procedures
Strictly regulated by state law. General process:
1. Notice to cure or quit (3-day to 30-day depending on violation and state)
2. Unlawful detainer filing if tenant doesn't cure
3. Court hearing
4. Judgment and writ of possession
5. Sheriff executes eviction

**Self-help eviction is ILLEGAL in all states:** Landlord cannot change locks, remove belongings, shut off utilities, or physically remove tenant without court order.

### Required Disclosures (Common)
- Lead-based paint (pre-1978 housing — federal requirement)
- Mold/mildew disclosure (some states)
- Sex offender registry notification (some states)
- Flooding/natural hazard zone (some states)
- Bed bug history (some cities: NYC, ME)
- Move-in/move-out condition checklist (many states require or recommend)
- Landlord/manager contact information
- Location of security deposit (bank name, account — some states)
- Tenant rights pamphlet (some states provide government-issued pamphlet)

## Professional Services

### Standard of Care
Professional services contracts imply a duty to perform services with the skill, care, and diligence customarily exercised by qualified practitioners in the same field and geographic area.

**Contract language to avoid:**
- "Best efforts" or "highest standard of care" — sets an unreasonably high bar
- "Perfect results" or "guarantee of outcome" — professionals cannot guarantee outcomes
- Better: "Services shall be performed in a professional and workmanlike manner consistent with generally accepted practices in the industry"

### Scope Creep Prevention
Professional service agreements should clearly define:
- Specific deliverables included
- Services explicitly excluded from scope
- Process for requesting additional services
- Pricing for out-of-scope work (hourly rate, fixed quote, change order)
- Approval required before out-of-scope work begins

### Client Responsibilities
Professional service agreements should clearly document client obligations:
- Timely provision of information and documents
- Access to systems and personnel
- Review and approval within specified timeframes
- Designated point of contact with authority to make decisions
- Consequences of client delay (timeline extension, additional fees)

### Retainer Arrangements
Common in professional services:
- **Earned retainer:** Payment for services already rendered (non-refundable)
- **Unearned retainer:** Advance payment for future services (held in trust, refundable for unused portion)
- **Evergreen retainer:** Auto-replenishing retainer — when balance drops below threshold, client is invoiced to restore to full amount
- **Flat monthly retainer:** Fixed monthly fee for ongoing services regardless of hours worked
