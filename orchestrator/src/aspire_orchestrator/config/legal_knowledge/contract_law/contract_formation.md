# Contract Formation

## Elements of a Valid Contract

### Offer
An offer is a clear, definite proposal made by one party (offeror) to another (offeree) indicating willingness to enter a binding agreement on stated terms.

**Requirements for a valid offer:**
- Must be communicated to the offeree
- Must contain definite and certain terms (parties, subject matter, price, time for performance)
- Must manifest intent to be bound upon acceptance
- Must be distinguished from an invitation to treat (advertisement, price list, catalog)

**Termination of offers:**
- Revocation by offeror (effective upon receipt by offeree)
- Rejection by offeree
- Counteroffer (operates as rejection + new offer)
- Lapse of time (reasonable time if no deadline specified)
- Death or incapacity of either party
- Destruction of subject matter
- Supervening illegality

**Firm offers (UCC 2-205):** A merchant's written offer to buy or sell goods, stating it will be held open, is irrevocable for the stated period (max 3 months) even without consideration.

### Acceptance
Acceptance is an unqualified expression of assent to the terms of an offer.

**Rules of acceptance:**
- Must be communicated to the offeror (silence is NOT acceptance, except in limited circumstances)
- Must mirror the offer exactly (mirror image rule at common law)
- Must be made by the person to whom the offer was directed
- Mailbox rule: acceptance effective upon dispatch (if using authorized medium)

**UCC modification (Battle of the Forms, 2-207):**
Under the UCC (sale of goods), an acceptance with additional or different terms is still valid as acceptance. Additional terms become part of the contract between merchants unless they materially alter the offer, the offer expressly limits acceptance, or objection is given within reasonable time.

### Consideration
Consideration is something of value exchanged between parties — a bargained-for exchange.

**Valid consideration includes:**
- Money or property
- Performance of services
- Promise to perform (or refrain from performing) an act
- Forbearance (giving up a legal right)

**Not valid consideration:**
- Past consideration (something already done before the promise)
- Pre-existing duty (promising to do what you're already obligated to do)
- Illusory promises (reserving unlimited right to back out)
- Moral obligation alone (gratitude, love)

**Adequacy:** Courts generally do not inquire into adequacy of consideration — a peppercorn suffices. Exception: gross inadequacy may indicate fraud, duress, or unconscionability.

### Capacity
Both parties must have legal capacity to enter a contract.

**Parties who lack capacity:**
- Minors (under 18 in most states) — contracts are voidable by the minor
- Mentally incapacitated persons — contracts are voidable if the person could not understand the nature and consequences
- Intoxicated persons — contracts are voidable if the other party knew of the intoxication
- Entities acting outside their authority (e.g., officer exceeding corporate authority)

**Minor's contracts:** Voidable, not void. Minor can ratify upon reaching majority. Minor must return consideration if possible when disaffirming (except necessities).

### Legality
The contract's subject matter and purpose must be legal. Contracts for illegal purposes are void and unenforceable.

**Common illegality issues:**
- Contracts to commit crimes or torts
- Gambling contracts (except where legalized)
- Usurious interest rates (varies by state)
- Contracts in restraint of trade (unless reasonable non-compete)
- Unlicensed professional services (in some states, the contract is void; in others, voidable)

## Statute of Frauds

### Contracts That Must Be in Writing
The Statute of Frauds requires certain contracts to be evidenced by a writing signed by the party to be charged:

1. **Marriage** — Contracts made in consideration of marriage (prenuptial agreements)
2. **Year** — Contracts that cannot be performed within one year from the date of making
3. **Land** — Contracts for the sale or transfer of an interest in land
4. **Executor** — Promise by an executor/administrator to pay estate debts from personal funds
5. **Guarantor** — Promise to answer for the debt of another (suretyship)
6. **Goods over $500** — UCC 2-201: contracts for sale of goods $500 or more (proposed UCC revision: $5,000)

**What satisfies "writing" under Statute of Frauds:**
- Signed paper document
- Email chain showing agreement and signed by party to be charged
- Text messages (courts increasingly accepting)
- Electronic signature meeting ESIGN/UETA requirements

### One-Year Rule Details
The one-year provision applies ONLY when the contract CANNOT possibly be performed within one year. If there is ANY possibility of completion within a year, the contract need not be in writing.

**Examples:**
- "Employment for 2 years" — must be in writing (cannot be performed in 1 year)
- "Employment for life" — need NOT be in writing (person could die within a year)
- "Paint my house" — need NOT be in writing (can be completed in 1 year)

## Electronic Signatures (ESIGN Act & UETA)

### ESIGN Act (Federal, 2000)
The Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act provides:
- Electronic signatures have the same legal effect as handwritten signatures
- Electronic records satisfy any law requiring a "writing"
- Consumer consent required before using electronic records (opt-in)
- Records must be accessible and retainable by the consumer

**ESIGN does NOT apply to:**
- Wills, codicils, testamentary trusts
- Family law (adoption, divorce, custody)
- UCC Articles 3-9 (negotiable instruments, except 2 and 2A)
- Court orders, notices, official court documents
- Cancellation of utility services
- Default, acceleration, foreclosure notices
- Health/life insurance cancellation
- Product recall notices

### UETA (State-Level)
The Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (adopted by 49 states + DC; New York has ESRA instead):
- Electronic signature = "electronic sound, symbol, or process" attached to a record with intent to sign
- Parties must consent to conduct transactions electronically
- Electronic record satisfies any rule of law requiring a writing
- Attribution: electronic signature attributable to a person if it was the act of the person (or their agent)

## Mutual Assent

### Meeting of the Minds
Both parties must actually agree to the same terms. Mistakes in understanding can void a contract.

**Unilateral mistake:** Generally does not void a contract unless the other party knew or should have known of the mistake.

**Mutual mistake:** If both parties are mistaken about a material fact at the time of contracting, the contract is voidable by the adversely affected party.

**Misunderstanding:** If the parties attach materially different meanings to a material term and neither party knows (or has reason to know) of the misunderstanding, no contract is formed.

## Defenses to Formation

### Duress
A contract is voidable if a party's assent was induced by improper threat that left no reasonable alternative. Includes physical threats, economic duress (threatening to breach an existing contract to extract new terms), and undue influence (exploiting a position of trust).

### Fraud / Misrepresentation
A contract is voidable if a party's assent was induced by a fraudulent or material misrepresentation. Requires: false statement of fact (not opinion), materiality, reliance, and damages.

### Unconscionability
A court may refuse to enforce a contract or clause that is so one-sided as to be unconscionable. Two types:
- **Procedural:** Unfair surprise, hidden terms, unequal bargaining power, no opportunity to review
- **Substantive:** Unreasonably favorable terms for one party, extreme price, harsh penalties

Both types typically must be present for a court to find unconscionability.
