## Lease vs Buy Decision Framework

Every equipment decision over $10,000 deserves a lease-vs-buy analysis. The right answer depends on utilization, tax situation, and cash position.

### Buy when
- **Utilization exceeds 70%** of available working hours (equipment will be used heavily and consistently)
- **Equipment has a long useful life** (10+ years for trucks, 7+ years for heavy equipment)
- **You have cash reserves** and buying does not drop you below 60 days of operating expenses
- **Section 179 deduction** is valuable to your tax situation (see below)
- **The asset appreciates or holds value** (land, certain specialty equipment)

### Lease when
- **Technology changes rapidly** (computers, diagnostic equipment, some power tools)
- **Utilization is seasonal or intermittent** (under 50% of available hours)
- **Cash preservation is critical** (growth phase, tight margins, seasonal cash flow)
- **You want to upgrade regularly** (every 3-5 years to maintain reliability)
- **Off-balance-sheet treatment** matters for bonding or lending applications

### Decision matrix
| Factor | Favors buying | Favors leasing |
|--------|--------------|----------------|
| Utilization | Over 70% | Under 50% |
| Useful life | 7+ years | Under 5 years |
| Cash position | Strong (90+ days reserves) | Tight (under 60 days) |
| Tax benefit needed | Yes (Section 179) | Less important |
| Technology obsolescence | Low risk | High risk |
| Maintenance | Predictable | Unpredictable (lease may include maintenance) |

## SBA Loans for Equipment and Expansion

### SBA 7(a) Loan Program
- **Maximum:** $5,000,000
- **Use for:** Equipment, working capital, inventory, real estate, refinancing
- **Terms:** Up to 10 years for equipment, up to 25 years for real estate
- **Interest rates (2025-2026):** Prime + 2.25-2.75% for loans over $50K (effective rate: 9.75-11.25%)
- **Down payment:** Typically 10-20%
- **Guarantee fee:** 2-3.75% of guaranteed portion
- **Approval timeline:** 30-90 days (can be faster through preferred lenders)
- **Best for:** Established businesses with 2+ years of history and strong cash flow

### SBA 504 Loan Program
- **Maximum:** $5,500,000 (up to $16.5M for energy-related projects)
- **Use for:** Real estate and major equipment purchases (fixed assets only, not working capital)
- **Structure:** 50% bank loan + 40% CDC/SBA loan + 10% borrower down payment
- **Interest rates (2025-2026):** CDC portion is below market at 5.5-6.5% fixed for 10-20 years
- **Advantage:** Lowest down payment (10%) and longest fixed rates available to small businesses
- **Best for:** Purchasing commercial property or equipment over $100,000

### SBA Microloan Program
- **Maximum:** $50,000
- **Average loan size:** $13,000
- **Interest rates:** 8-13%
- **Terms:** Up to 6 years
- **Best for:** Startups and very small businesses that cannot qualify for conventional financing

### Financing sources

2. **Banks and credit unions:** Best rates for established businesses with strong banking relationships
3. **Online lenders (Balboa Capital, Crest Capital, National Funding):** Faster approval, higher rates
4. **Equipment leasing companies (GATX, CIT):** Specialize in lease structures
5. **Manufacturer financing:** CAT Financial, John Deere Financial, CASE — competitive rates for their equipment

## When to Finance vs Pay Cash

### The ROI rule of thumb
**Finance when the expected ROI on the equipment exceeds the interest rate on the financing.**

Example: A jetter machine costs $35,000 and is expected to generate $6,500/month in net revenue. Annual ROI = ($6,500 x 12) / $35,000 = 223%. Equipment financing at 8% is a clear win — finance and keep the $35,000 working in the business.

### Pay cash when
- The equipment costs under $5,000 (financing overhead is not worth it for small purchases)
- You have surplus cash above 90 days of operating expenses
- Interest rates exceed 15% (at that point, financing cost erodes margins)
- The purchase does not meaningfully reduce cash reserves

### Finance when
- The purchase would drop cash reserves below 60 days of operating expenses
- You can get rates under 10% with your credit profile
- The equipment directly generates revenue (revenue-producing assets should be financed to preserve cash for operations)
- Dealer is offering promotional financing (0% for 12-24 months)

### Never do
- Finance equipment on credit cards (18-28% APR destroys the economics)
- Take a personal loan for business equipment (creates liability exposure and loses tax benefits)
- Finance equipment you cannot utilize at least 50% of available hours

## Tax Benefits: Section 179 and Depreciation

### Section 179 Deduction (2025-2026)
- **Maximum deduction:** $1,220,000 (2025, adjusts annually for inflation)
- **Phase-out threshold:** Begins at $3,050,000 in total equipment purchases
- **What qualifies:** Tangible personal property used for business (vehicles, equipment, machinery, computers, software)
- **Key benefit:** Deduct the full purchase price in the year of acquisition instead of depreciating over multiple years
- **Example:** Purchase a $50,000 truck. Section 179 lets you deduct the full $50,000 in year one, reducing taxable income by $50,000. At a 24% tax rate, that is $12,000 in tax savings.

### Bonus Depreciation (2025-2026)
- **Current rate:** 40% bonus depreciation in 2025 (stepped down from 100% in 2022, decreasing 20% per year)
- **2026 rate:** 20% bonus depreciation
- **Applies to:** New and used equipment placed in service during the tax year
- **Strategy:** Combine Section 179 and bonus depreciation for maximum first-year deduction

### Vehicle-specific limits (2025)
- **Passenger vehicles (under 6,000 lbs GVWR):** Section 179 limited to $12,400 first year
- **Heavy vehicles (over 6,000 lbs GVWR):** Full Section 179 deduction up to $28,900 for SUVs, no limit for trucks and vans
- **This is why trades businesses favor heavy-duty trucks** — the tax treatment is dramatically more favorable

### Leasing tax treatment
- Lease payments are fully deductible as a business expense in the year they are paid
- No depreciation schedule to track
- Simpler accounting but no asset on the balance sheet
- Operating leases keep debt off the balance sheet (important for bonding capacity and bank covenants)

### Consult your accountant
Tax strategy for equipment purchases depends on your specific income, entity type, and state tax rules. The numbers above are general guidance — always verify with your CPA before making purchases based on tax assumptions. A $50,000 equipment purchase decision should include a 15-minute call with your accountant.
